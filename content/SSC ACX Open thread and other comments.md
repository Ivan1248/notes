[Comment](https://open.substack.com/pub/astralcodexten/p/tegmarks-mathematical-universe-defeats?r=1c0bzf&utm_campaign=comment-list-share-cta&utm_medium=web&comments=true&commentId=94879778) by Igon Value:
> "Splitting" is just a way to describe the situation in English. If you want to understand this, you need to look at the math (apologies if that sounds patronizing).
>
> Let's say we have an electron in a superposition of states.
>
> |E1> = |0> + |1>
>
> (Maybe 2 energy states, or position, spin, whatever; it could be infinitely more too, they need to be normalized, and so on, but let's keep it simple.)
>
> We know this state is possible, I'm not cheating.
>
> Now, let's say we have another electron, also in a superposition of states:
>
> |E2> = |0> + |1>
>
> These are different |0> and |1> vectors because that's a different electron, but again let's ignore the subscripts for clarity's sake.
>
> OK, now we are concerned with the total state made of both electrons together. QM gives us the same way to describe that composite state as it did for both states separately, i.e. the full state will be a linear combination of all possible classical states:
>
> |E1+E2> = |00> + |01> + |10> + |11>
>
> where |xy> means E1 in state |x> and E2 in state |y> and there should be a complex coefficient in front of each term.
>
> Now it is possible that |E1+E2> = (|0> + |1>)(|0> + |1>) in which case the electrons simply don't interact (measuring E1 has no bearing on E2). But a more interesting case (among others) is this, where the coefficients for the cross-terms are null:
>
> |E1+E2> = |00> + |11>
>
> We know this state is possible too, I'm still not cheating. However, it is worth noting that Bohr and Heisenberg didn't know this was possible in the 1920s. Einstein progressively understood it but it was only validated experimentally in 1945 by C.S. Wu.
>
> Anyway, in this entangled states both electrons are separately in a superposition (as before), but measuring the state of E1 gives you the state of E2 with 100% confidence (in theory). In this state, we have two "branches", one in which E1 and E2 are in the states |0>, and one where they are in the states |1>.
>
> Would you say that there are two worlds? One where E1 and E2 are in states |0> and one where they are in states |1>? Would you mention "splitting" to describe the branches? Maybe. That's just a way of describing the situation in English.
>
> None of that is outside the standard formalism of QM, by the way. The interpretation starts when "someone" measures E1 or E2 or E1+E2. Copenhagen says that the measurement itself destroys the superposition and only one component remains. Many-worlds says that what happens is that observer O gets entangled with the system exactly like E2 got entangled above. There is no difference between E2 being an electron and E2 being an electron that happens to be part of a measuring apparatus with a physicist on the other end.
>
> The Copenhagen interpretation has a major problem with the entangled states |E1+E2> above. Because it is the observation of E1 that causes the collapse of both |E1> and |E2>, it becomes necessary for information to travel from E1 to E2 instantaneously no matter how far E1 is from E2. (This was also validated experimentally in 1982 by Alain Aspect.) The proponents of the interpretation just say "oh well, no big deal, the theory is just not local, but you could not use that anyway" (well, duh!).
>
> The many-worlds interpretation doesn't have this issue because when you measure E1, you just learn something about which world you are in: the one where E1 is in state |0> or the one where it is in state |1>. And knowing in what world you're in also tells you what measuring E2 will produce. No need for information to travel to E2.
>
> As to how many "worlds" ("branches") there can be, well, far more than 10^80, in fact possibly an infinity, even possibly an uncountable infinity. As I said before, it is like adding a dimension. Imagine that we had another dimension of time, that would imply an uncountable infinite number of parallel "universes", where a "universe" is akin to the universe we experience, in the same way that the dimension of time we already have implies an infinite number of 3-d spaces.
>
> Describing all of this in English can be very misleading.

From [Mallard](https://substack.com/profile/131523566-mallard?utm_source=substack-feed-item):
> If you save the kid, then your one act made a real difference and you feel good about what you’ve done
>
> Right. Saving a drowning kid obviously _feels_ different from donating to malaria relief in Africa. That's the whole point of the thought experiment!
>
> To consider the ways in which the latter is conceptually similar to the former, in spite of feeling different. Quantifying the elements which cause the former to feel different from the latter isn't sufficient to address the thought experiment.
>
> A drowning kid _feels_ urgent, while a kid dying of malaria doesn't feel urgent, but in reality, the latter is roughly similarly urgent. A thousand kids, or so, die a day from drowning, and a thousand kids, or so, die a day from malaria. While you can only save the particular kid from drowning today, and you have a little longer to save particular kids from malaria (maybe a couple of months until the next batch of nets are purchased or distributed), you can be quite certain that if you don't donate to malaria nets within that window that outside donations won't replace them (this is clear as malaria prevention remains underfunded).
>
> The point of the thought experiment is to get the listener to consider: if saving kids is so important, does it really matter if I need to act within a minute or within a month?
>
> True, saving a kid is a one off, but donating to save a kid can also be a one off. It's probably less satisfying, since it's easy to think about the other kids that will still die of malaria, but you could do the same thing when saving a drowning kid. In spite of saving the drowning kid, many other kids will still drown that day. You can "reassure yourself" that you can't save the other drowning kids, which may make it more "satisfying," but if you'd feel an imperative to save the drowning kid, rather than just a source of satisfaction, then you should similarly feel an imperative to save kids from malaria. And if there's no imperative for the latter, since you can't save them all, why should there be an imperative for the former?
>
> The point of the thought experiment is that the average person would save a kid, even at the cost of a few thousand, if they could see the kid and feel satisfied about it. Indeed, the average person would even feel an imperative to. Similarly, one should feel an imperative to donate to save kids from malaria. But the "refutation" about devoting oneself wholly to saving kids from drowning doesn't hold up.
>
> If someone had to save kids from drowning all the time, then the marginal cost per saved life would increase, due to losing their job, etc. Just as a person probably wouldn't feel an imperative to do that, a person could reasonably not feel an imperative to save so many kids from malaria that the marginal cost of saving them starts increasing (since marginal dollars have substantially higher value when a person is poorer).
>
> That's not an argument, however, against saving a small number of children from malaria, such that the marginal cost wouldn't be greater than the few thousand dollars one would lost from a suit.
>
> Furthermore, the thought experiment is useful even if one doesn't focus on the act being an imperative, but simply of the impact of the act.
>
> Ignoring imperatives, and thinking about how a charitable act makes a donor feel, it's hardly trivial that a life can be saved for the cost of a few thousand dollars. Since many people would be very happy to save a life for a few thousand dollars if it involved a drowning kid, for example, a potential donor can examine their own feelings and consider whether they might be similarly motivated to save far away kids for the same cost. As noted, people don't feel this way naturally. But thinking about the thought experiment can help people identify what they fundamentally value and potentially inspire them to shift their behavior accordingly. E.g. if they decide that they don't care about Africans, at all, and if they saw a kid drowning, while wearing a shirt reading "African temporarily in the US," that they wouldn't bother saving them, then fine. Hopefully pondering the thought experiment sharpens the person's values.
>
> If a person thinks that they would save a drowning African, but not a far away one, they could think about variant thought experiments. What if a kid is drowning in a pool in Africa, but you could make a very expensive phone call to a local to save him?
>
> Simply noting that the examples feel different misses the point. The thought experiment can be useful in helping isolate the variables of what a person cares about and helping them act accordingly.
