This is a proposal to simplify language rules and reduce the number of different dashes.

Meaning of symbols:
- $W$ – word,
- $S$ – sentence or phrase.

| Name                        | Symbol     | Standard usage                      | Replacement proposal     | Example usages                                   |
| --------------------------- | ---------- | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------ |
| hyphen                      | "-"        | "$W$-$W$"                           | "$W$-$W$"                | "well-known", "up-to-date"                       |
| unspaced en dash            | "–"        | "$W$–$W$"                           | "$W$–$W$" or "$W$ - $W$" | "2024–2042", "London–Paris route", "pages 10–20" |
| unspaced and spaced em dash | "—", " – " | "$S$—$S$", "$S$ — $S$", "$S$ – $S$" | "$S$ – $S$"              | "She was tired – exhausted, really."             |
| em dash for attribution     | "— "       | "— $S$"                             | "– $S$"                  | "– Bertrand Russel"                              |

## Keyboard shortcut

I set `AltGr`+`-` to produce the en-dash.

## Examples

A spaced en dash can be used to mark a break in a sentence, and to set off a [[Parenthetical phrase|parenthetical phrase]]. Example:
> He has two sons – one of them is older, and the other one is younger.

An unspaced en dash can indicates spans or differentiation, where it may replace "and", "from – to", or "through". Example:
> 2024–2042

An n-dash could be instead of an m-dash for attributing a source to a direct quotation:
>Let us go and respire the air.  
>– Pedro Carolino
